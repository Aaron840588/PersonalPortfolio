# Portfolio strategy

## Strategy in one sentence

Present Aaron as an operations-and-systems candidate with implementation fluency through a calm, recruiter-scannable portfolio where professional experience establishes trust, two real projects provide the strongest visual evidence, and technology supports the story without becoming his identity.

## Audience and positioning

The primary audience is recruiters and hiring managers screening entry-level roles in IT operations, application or systems support, MIS, QA/UAT, systems analysis, cloud support, and business technology. Technical reviewers are a secondary audience who may inspect repositories, implementation details, and testing evidence after the professional story is clear.

Use this professional label consistently:

**IT Operations · Systems · Business Technology**

The core value proposition is:

**I make operational systems easier to understand, support, test, and improve.**

Lead with workflows, documentation, requirements, testing, data, support, and cloud platforms. Present frameworks and programming languages as implementation tools. Do not position Aaron as senior, an expert, a Software Architect, an AI Engineer, a Cloud Engineer, or a Full-Stack Engineer by profession.

## The 30-second recruiter test

The first viewport and one short scroll should answer:

1. Who is Aaron and which roles fit him?
2. What professional evidence supports that direction?
3. Which two systems best demonstrate his judgment?
4. What did he personally contribute?
5. How can a recruiter inspect his work or contact him?

If a section repeats one of those answers without adding evidence, remove or merge it.

## Five-section homepage hierarchy

### 01 — Hero

Keep the hero compact and typographic:

- Aaron Tagapan.
- IT Operations · Systems · Business Technology.
- One restrained headline and one short supporting paragraph.
- View Work and Download Résumé as the primary actions.
- Quiet GitHub and LinkedIn links.
- A small location or availability line if it improves clarity.

Do not include a system map, profile rail, vCard sidebar, framework list, fabricated metric, profile image, phone number, floating graphic, or repeated positioning statement.

### 02 — Selected work

Projects are the dominant visual section and should appear immediately after the hero. Use two large editorial features, not a card grid. Each feature includes:

- Project name and one-line description.
- One large, real, sanitized screenshot.
- Brief Problem, What I built, and My role summaries.
- A short technology line.
- Case study, repository, and live-demo actions where currently valid.

H+H Hub appears first because it shows the greatest systems depth. Kat's Pet-Care CRM receives the clearer live-demo affordance. Additional projects may appear only as one quiet text row after the two primary projects; they must not compete visually with them.

### 03 — Experience

Gardenia Bakeries Philippines, Inc. is the only professional-experience entry and should be unmistakable as employment experience rather than project work. Show:

- Business Analyst Intern — MIS.
- December 2025 to March 2026.
- 486 hours and Biñan location.
- Three concise responsibilities centered on requirements, process analysis, documentation, and QA/UAT.

Avoid a decorative timeline. Use a simple stacked record with thin dividers.

### 04 — Capabilities and background

Use plain categorized text lists rather than pills, meters, logo clouds, or icon grids:

- **IT Operations:** Windows, hardware diagnostics, driver management, VirtualBox, Linux fundamentals.
- **Systems & Cloud:** Supabase, Firebase, Vercel, PostgreSQL, REST APIs, GitHub.
- **Business Systems:** Requirements, process flows, FSDs, UAT, QA, data modeling.
- **Applications & Automation:** relevant implementation and automation tools supported by the résumé or repositories.

Follow with a compact education and training list. Education remains factual; certifications and training stay visually secondary. Do not add TOEIC without a verified score and date.

### 05 — Contact

Close with one short invitation and direct links for email, LinkedIn, GitHub, and résumé download. A backend contact form is unnecessary. Keep the footer minimal and do not repeat the navigation, biography, or positioning statement at length.

## Five-section case-study hierarchy

Both project pages use the same concise structure so reviewers can compare them quickly.

### 01 — Project hero

Show the project name, system type, period/status, one-line description, repository and currently valid demo actions, a quiet availability disclosure, and one large screenshot. Metadata should read as a simple row, not badges or statistic cards.

### 02 — Project summary

Use a restrained three-column or stacked layout:

1. **Problem** — the operational coordination, data, or support problem.
2. **What I built** — one coherent system response rather than a feature dump.
3. **My role** — Aaron's verified responsibilities and decisions.

This replaces separate context, system-response, users, contribution, and evidence-rail sections.

### 03 — Selected workflows

Use two supporting screenshots at generous size with short captions explaining what workflow is visible and why it matters. Select only the strongest workflows. Do not place screenshots inside fake browser chrome, layered devices, heavy shadows, or rounded dashboard cards.

### 04 — Implementation and reliability

Present technologies as simple searchable text. Pair them with four to six high-value implementation, testing, reliability, or safeguard points. Prefer exact checks and bounded claims over exhaustive framework lists.

### 05 — Tradeoffs and next step

Summarize two or three meaningful tradeoffs or lessons, show the evidence boundary as a quiet footnote, and finish with one next-project link plus a restrained contact action. Remove decorative system diagrams, numbered node maps, and repeated calls to action.

## Content reduction rules

- State the professional positioning once in the hero and support it with evidence elsewhere.
- Prefer one strong sentence over a headline plus two paragraphs saying the same thing.
- Keep three to five points per content group.
- Remove the evidence ledger, systems-behind-the-work explanation, decorative system maps, repeated metric rails, excessive indexes, skill pills, and duplicated project summaries.
- Do not turn test counts or route counts into business-impact metrics.
- Do not invent revenue, adoption, time savings, project scale, employers, private clients, credentials, or outcomes.
- Preserve useful alt text and concise captions; do not hide essential information inside screenshots.

## Visual and motion direction

Use a soft dark-neutral canvas, one centered column around 1100–1200px, Geist Sans, Geist Mono only for small metadata, thin dividers, measured whitespace, and a limited type scale. Use the pale accent sparingly for focus, a small status dot, and selected link states.

Avoid bright neon, gradients, glows, glassmorphism, heavy shadows, large rounded cards, decorative grid backgrounds, fake browser chrome, floating nodes, excessive icons, and oversized hero text.

Retain only:

- A soft fade-up on section entry.
- A small image reveal.
- Subtle link and navigation transitions.
- A thin page-progress indicator.

The page must remain complete with motion disabled. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`, avoid continuous movement, and animate only opacity or small transforms.

## Accessibility and performance

- Use semantic landmarks, one H1 per route, sequential headings, descriptive links, and a skip link.
- Preserve visible focus states and approximately 44px interaction targets.
- Keep demo status and evidence boundaries visible in text rather than tooltips or color alone.
- Maintain DOM order that matches the visual reading order at every breakpoint.
- Support narrow reflow without horizontal page scrolling.
- Server-render factual content and keep client-side motion or interaction islands small.
- Use `next/image` with accurate dimensions and `sizes`; support desktop and mobile screenshot aspect ratios without forced letterboxing.
- Treat LCP ≤2.5s, INP ≤200ms, and CLS ≤0.1 as targets, not unmeasured claims.

## Truthfulness rules

1. Trace every meaningful title, date, metric, technology, repository, and demo action to [CONTENT_SOURCE.md](./CONTENT_SOURCE.md).
2. Distinguish résumé claims, repository evidence, and verified live behavior.
3. Treat deployment status as time-sensitive and reverify before changing availability language.
4. Use only reviewed, privacy-safe screenshots and synthetic records.
5. Omit unsupported claims instead of softening them with vague language.
6. Do not expose Aaron's phone number outside the downloadable résumé.
7. Explain AI only as a support for research, implementation, testing, debugging, documentation, or verification where accurate.

## Final review

- Can a recruiter identify Aaron's role direction before seeing a framework name?
- Are the two primary projects the strongest visual elements?
- Is Gardenia clearly professional experience?
- Can every section be scanned without reading repeated copy?
- Are H+H maintenance and Pet CRM demo behavior represented accurately?
- Does each case study clearly show Problem, What I built, My role, selected workflows, implementation, and evidence boundaries?
- Can keyboard and reduced-motion users reach every action and all meaningful content?
- Can a skeptical reviewer trace every material claim to a source?
